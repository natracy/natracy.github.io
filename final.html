Introduction

The Washington Metropolitan Area Transit Authority was founded on February 20, 1967 to address the transit needs of the Capital City. WMATA (as it’s colloquially known) has the unique challenge of managing a bus and rail system in Washington, DC, Virginia, and Maryland. This creates funding circumstances of three jurisdictions in addition to veto power by the US Congress due to Washington, DC being a federal district. Creating and funding a functional system has been an ongoing dispute for a variety of reasons including poor union leadership, ineffective management, and jurisdictional hold up. As the Washington DC metro area population continues to grow and the metrorail system continues to expand, support of the rail system is necessary for regional success.

To solve, I propose measuring ridership in weekday boardings per jurisdiction. There is no way to segregate riders per line due to every line overlapping except the Red Line. Therefore, I have grouped the stations into Washington, DC, Virginia, and Maryland. Public transit is most often used by commuters which I will prove by analyzing Washington, DC unemployment rates versus Metro ridership. Interestingly enough, the data supports higher unemployment in Washington, DC even when DC ridership levels are at their highest. This highlights the number of commuters traveling into the city from the surrounding jurisdictions.

As the Washington, DC population grows along with higher than average national unemployment rates, DC stations saw significant growth. This can only lead to the conclusion that riders are entering the system from the suburbs and commuting into the city for work. This evidence should allow the discussion to begin about sensible tri-jurisdictional management and funding more evenly spread throughout the three governments rather than relying on DC as the major contributor.
